Scalar Irrecoverability and the Geometric Structure of Behavioral Anomalies

Andrew H. Bond
Department of Computer Engineering, San José State University
March 2026

Abstract

We prove that projecting a $d$-dimensional attribute vector ($d \geq 2$) to a scalar utility value destroys information that is mathematically irrecoverable: no continuous function can invert the projection. This scalar irrecoverability theorem establishes that scalar utility theory is not merely an imprecise approximation of multi-attribute decision-making—the information it discards cannot be reconstructed from the scalar output regardless of the sophistication of the utility function.

We then show that three foundational "anomalies" of behavioral economics—the Allais paradox (violation of independence), the Ellsberg paradox (ambiguity aversion), and intransitive preferences—arise as necessary consequences of scalar projection from a multi-dimensional attribute space in which preferences are fully transitive and consistent. The anomalies are projection artifacts, not cognitive errors. This result reframes the relationship between classical and behavioral economics: the two are not competing theories of the same phenomenon but descriptions at different dimensionalities of the same underlying decision manifold.

Keywords: scalar utility, irrecoverability, dimensional projection, Allais paradox, Ellsberg paradox, intransitivity, behavioral economics, attribute space, Brouwer invariance

1. Introduction

The expected utility framework (von Neumann & Morgenstern, 1944; Savage, 1954) represents preferences by a scalar function $U : X \to \mathbb{R}$ such that option $x$ is preferred to $y$ if and only if $U(x) > U(y)$. The existence of such a function requires that preferences satisfy completeness, transitivity, independence, and continuity. Decades of experimental evidence have shown that human preferences systematically violate these axioms.

The standard interpretation is that humans are "boundedly rational" or "biased"—they approximate but fail to achieve the ideal of scalar utility maximization. We offer a different interpretation grounded in a mathematical impossibility result: the axiom violations are necessary consequences of evaluating a multi-dimensional attribute space through a one-dimensional lens. The "biases" are not in the agent; they are in the projection.

2. The Scalar Irrecoverability Theorem

Theorem 1 (Scalar Irrecoverability). Let $\mathbf{a} : X \to \mathbb{R}^d$ assign a $d$-dimensional attribute vector to each alternative $x \in X$, where $d \geq 2$. Let $\pi : \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}$ be any continuous function mapping attribute vectors to scalar utility. Then:

  1. $\pi$ is not injective: there exist distinct attribute vectors $\mathbf{a} \neq \mathbf{a}'$ with $\pi(\mathbf{a}) = \pi(\mathbf{a}')$.
  2. The information lost under $\pi$ is irrecoverable: there exists no continuous function $\rho : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}^d$ such that $\rho \circ \pi = \mathrm{id}_{\mathbb{R}^d}$.
  3. The optimal path on the full attribute space is in general different from the path that maximizes $\pi$-utility, and the difference is not bounded by any function of $\pi$ alone.

Proof

(1) Suppose $\pi$ is injective on a compact subset $K \subset \mathbb{R}^d$. Then $\pi|_K : K \to \pi(K)$ is a continuous injection from a compact space to a Hausdorff space, hence a homeomorphism onto its image. But $K$ has topological dimension $d \geq 2$ while $\pi(K) \subset \mathbb{R}$ has topological dimension at most 1. By Brouwer's invariance of domain theorem, a homeomorphism preserves topological dimension—contradiction. Therefore $\pi$ is not injective.

(2) Since $\pi$ is not injective, there exist $\mathbf{a}_1 \neq \mathbf{a}_2$ with $\pi(\mathbf{a}_1) = \pi(\mathbf{a}_2) = c$. Any left inverse $\rho$ must satisfy $\rho(c) = \mathbf{a}_1$ and $\rho(c) = \mathbf{a}_2$, which is impossible since $\mathbf{a}_1 \neq \mathbf{a}_2$.

(3) Consider two actions with identical $\pi$-values ($\pi(\Delta\mathbf{a}_1) = \pi(\Delta\mathbf{a}_2)$) but different attribute-vector changes. Their Mahalanobis costs $\sqrt{\Delta\mathbf{a}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \Delta\mathbf{a}}$ can differ arbitrarily depending on $\Sigma$, so the $\pi$-optimal and manifold-optimal paths can diverge without bound. $\square$

3. Behavioral Anomalies as Projection Artifacts

3.1 The Allais Paradox

In Allais's (1953) experiment, subjects choose between: Gamble A ($1M certain) vs. Gamble B (89% chance of $1M, 10% chance of $5M, 1% chance of $0). Most prefer A. They then choose between: Gamble C (11% chance of $1M, 89% chance of $0) vs. Gamble D (10% chance of $5M, 90% chance of $0). Most prefer D. This violates the independence axiom of expected utility.

Proposition 1 (Allais as Projection). In the two-dimensional attribute space $(a_1, a_9)$ where $a_1$ is monetary expected value and $a_9$ is epistemic certainty (confidence in the outcome), the preference reversal is consistent:

  • A vs. B: $\mathbf{a}_A = (1M, 1.0)$ vs. $\mathbf{a}_B = (1.39M, \, p)$ where $p < 1$. With sufficient weight on $a_9$ in $\Sigma^{-1}$, the Mahalanobis distance favors A.
  • C vs. D: $\mathbf{a}_C = (0.11M, \, q_1)$ vs. $\mathbf{a}_D = (0.50M, \, q_2)$ where $q_1 \approx q_2$ (both are gambles; certainty difference is small). The monetary dimension dominates, favoring D.
The independence axiom is violated on the scalar projection $\pi = a_1$ but satisfied on the full $(a_1, a_9)$ space: preferences are consistent given that the certainty premium ($a_9$) matters in the first pair but not the second.

3.2 The Ellsberg Paradox

Proposition 2 (Ellsberg as Projection). Ambiguity aversion—preferring known to unknown probabilities even with identical expected values—arises directly from the epistemic dimension $a_9$. Known probabilities have $a_9 > 0$; ambiguous probabilities have $a_9 \approx 0$. The Mahalanobis distance from the ambiguous option to the goal is larger even when $a_1$ (expected monetary value) is equal, because $\Sigma^{-1}$ assigns positive weight to the epistemic dimension.

3.3 Intransitive Preferences

Theorem 2 (Projected Intransitivity). Let $\succ_{\mathcal{C}}$ denote preference on the full $d$-dimensional attribute space and $\succ_\pi$ denote preference on the scalar projection. Then:

  1. $\succ_{\mathcal{C}}$ is transitive (it is the total order induced by path cost from the current state).
  2. $\succ_\pi$ may be intransitive: there exist alternatives $x, y, z$ such that $x \succ_\pi y \succ_\pi z \succ_\pi x$.

Proof (1) Path cost on $\mathcal{C}$ is a real-valued function; the induced ordering is a total preorder, hence transitive. (2) Consider three alternatives with attribute vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$. If the "relevant" dimension shifts with the comparison pair—comparing $x$ vs. $y$ primarily on $(a_1, a_2)$, comparing $y$ vs. $z$ on $(a_2, a_3)$, and $z$ vs. $x$ on $(a_1, a_3)$—then the scalar projections can yield a cycle. Formally: let $\mathbf{a}_x = (3, 1, 2)$, $\mathbf{a}_y = (1, 3, 1)$, $\mathbf{a}_z = (2, 2, 3)$ and let $\pi_{xy}$, $\pi_{yz}$, $\pi_{zx}$ be context-dependent projections to the two "salient" dimensions for each pair. Then $\pi_{xy}(\mathbf{a}_x) > \pi_{xy}(\mathbf{a}_y)$ (x wins on dims 1,3), $\pi_{yz}(\mathbf{a}_y) > \pi_{yz}(\mathbf{a}_z)$ (y wins on dims 1,2), $\pi_{zx}(\mathbf{a}_z) > \pi_{zx}(\mathbf{a}_x)$ (z wins on dims 2,3). The cycle is an artifact of context-dependent projection; on $\mathcal{C}$ with a fixed metric, preferences are transitive. $\square$

4. Geometric Derivation of Prospect-Theoretic Phenomena

4.1 Loss Aversion

Proposition 3 (Loss Aversion as Asymmetric Dimensionality). Gains and losses are not symmetric on the attribute manifold. A monetary loss of $\delta$ activates additional dimensions beyond $a_1$: rights ($a_2$: "this was mine"), fairness ($a_3$: "this is unjust"), and identity ($a_7$: "I am worse off"). An equivalent gain activates only $a_1$. The Mahalanobis distance traversed by a loss is therefore strictly greater than the distance traversed by an equivalent gain: $$\mathrm{BF}(\text{loss of } \delta) = \sqrt{\Delta\mathbf{a}_{\text{loss}}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \Delta\mathbf{a}_{\text{loss}}} > \sqrt{\Delta\mathbf{a}_{\text{gain}}^\top \Sigma^{-1} \Delta\mathbf{a}_{\text{gain}}} = \mathrm{BF}(\text{gain of } \delta)$$ because $\|\Delta\mathbf{a}_{\text{loss}}\| > \|\Delta\mathbf{a}_{\text{gain}}\|$ (losses have nonzero components on more dimensions). The loss-aversion coefficient $\lambda \approx 2$ (Tversky & Kahneman, 1992) is therefore not a free parameter but a consequence of the dimensionality ratio: how many more attribute dimensions are activated by losses than by gains.

4.2 Reference Dependence

Remark 1. Reference dependence—evaluating outcomes as gains or losses relative to a reference point rather than in absolute terms—follows naturally from the path-cost formulation. The behavioral friction $\mathrm{BF}(v, v')$ is defined as the Mahalanobis distance between states, which is inherently relative: it measures the change in attribute vector, not the absolute position. The reference point is simply the current state $v$.

4.3 Probability Weighting

Proposition 4 (Probability Weighting as Epistemic Cost). The probability weighting function $w(p)$ that overweights small $p$ and underweights large $p$ (Tversky & Kahneman, 1992) arises from the epistemic dimension $a_9$. Small probabilities correspond to high epistemic uncertainty ($a_9$ is low), which increases the Mahalanobis distance to the goal. Large probabilities correspond to near-certainty ($a_9$ is high). The distortion of $w(p)$ from the identity function reflects the curvature of $a_9$ as a function of $p$, which is steep near 0 and 1 (where small changes in probability produce large changes in subjective certainty).

5. Discussion

The scalar irrecoverability theorem is a negative result with constructive implications. It tells us precisely what cannot be done: no scalar utility function, however cleverly parameterized, can represent the information content of multi-attribute decisions. This is not an approximation bound—it is an impossibility.

The constructive implication is that behavioral anomalies are not evidence against rational decision-making; they are evidence against scalar decision-making. The pattern of anomalies—independence violations when certainty is at stake (Allais), ambiguity aversion when epistemic confidence varies (Ellsberg), apparent intransitivity when different dimensions are salient (Tversky)—is precisely the pattern predicted by dimensional projection from a consistent multi-attribute space.

An empirically testable prediction follows: if the attribute dimensions and their correlations ($\Sigma$) can be measured independently (via multi-attribute preference elicitation), then the specific anomalies observed in choice experiments should be quantitatively predictable from the projection geometry, without fitting anomaly-specific parameters.

6. Conclusion

We have proved that scalar utility irrecoverably destroys multi-attribute information (Theorem 1), and that three foundational behavioral anomalies—the Allais paradox, the Ellsberg paradox, and intransitive preferences—are necessary consequences of this information loss (Propositions 1–2, Theorem 2). We have further shown that loss aversion, reference dependence, and probability weighting arise as geometric properties of the multi-attribute decision manifold (Propositions 3–4), not as free parameters of a descriptive model.

The message is that classical economics and behavioral economics are not competing accounts of decision-making. They are descriptions at different dimensionalities of the same underlying structure. Classical economics is the $d = 1$ slice; behavioral economics catalogues the anomalies that appear on that slice. A $d \geq 2$ theory resolves the anomalies without abandoning rationality.
