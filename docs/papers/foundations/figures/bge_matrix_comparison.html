Key insight: The Nash equilibrium (Offer $1, Accept) is the d1-only projection of the BGE (Theorem 20.3, part 2). When the manifold collapses to monetary return alone, the $1 offer appears optimal because the βfairness = 8.0 penalty on d3, the d5 = 4.0 trust cost, and the d7 = 5.0 identity cost are all projected to zero. The responder's rejection of low offers is not "irrational" — it is rational on the full manifold. The BGE correctly predicts the ~50% rejection rate for offers below 30% that Nash cannot explain.

Formal relationship: Nash = limβ→0, Σ→diag(1,0,...,0) BGE — Nash is the degenerate limit of BGE where all non-monetary dimensions are zeroed.

The Prisoner's "Dilemma" is an artifact of dimensional collapse. On d1 alone, (Defect, Defect) is the unique Nash equilibrium with BF = 1.0 each. But on the full manifold, defection incurs massive costs on d5 (trust destruction = 6.0–8.0) and d6 (social impact = 2.0–4.0) that make mutual cooperation the minimum-BF outcome.

The contraction lemma (Lemma 20.1) predicts: when the self-separation αi > cross-sensitivity κi (i.e., each agent's d5 cost is dominated by their own decision, not by the other's), mutual cooperation is the unique BGE. This condition holds in repeated interactions with stable social ties — explaining why cooperation is the norm in ongoing relationships and rare in one-shot anonymous interactions (where d5 weight approaches zero and BGE collapses back to Nash).
